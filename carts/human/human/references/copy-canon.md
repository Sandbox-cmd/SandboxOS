# Copy Canon

The evidence base for the language rules in this skill. Part 1 ranks the sources and says how each earned its place. Part 2 lists the rules that survived verification, organized by framework dimension. Claims the verifier killed sit at the bottom with the reason. Downgraded claims carry their caveat where they appear.

## Part 1: The Canon

Ranked by how much each source teaches about language itself, weighted by whether its claims were measured or merely believed. Sources that appeared under several lenses are merged into one entry.

**1. Robertson et al. 2023, "Negativity drives online news consumption" (Nature Human Behaviour), on the Upworthy Research Archive.** The largest headline experiment corpus in existence: 22,743 randomized trials, roughly 105,000 headline variations, 5.7 million clicks, 370 million impressions, reduced to per-word effect sizes. It teaches that single-word swaps are measurable levers. One added negative word raised click-through 2.3% on an average-length headline. Emotion category mattered more than valence: sadness words pulled clicks, fear words repelled them, anger words did nothing. Fully tested, and fully verified against the published paper. The one boundary: the outcome is 2013–2015 clickbait CTR rather than sales.

**2. John Caples, *Tested Advertising Methods*.** The only classic whose word lists came from counted coupons. Caples ran the same ad with one element changed and tallied replies for four decades, and his conclusions about language follow from those counts: self-interest appeals beat news, news beats curiosity, "free" and "new" pull, specifics beat generalities, cleverness costs sales. Tested in method, but the famous multipliers (3-to-1 on a headline change, one ad outselling another 19.5x) are one man's unaudited mail-order records with no data tables. Treat the direction as solid and the specific numbers as legend.

**3. Chip Heath and Dan Heath, *Made to Stick*.** The densest set of sentence-level rewrite operations in the persuasion literature: compress the core to proverb length, open with a curiosity gap, use sense-checkable nouns, restate statistics on a human scale, make claims the reader can test, carry ideas as one person doing one thing. The underlying mechanisms are peer-reviewed (concrete-noun recall, identifiable-victim effects, Loewenstein's gap theory). The SUCCESs packaging itself is asserted synthesis.

**4. The Cialdini experimental corpus (*Influence* plus Langer et al. 1978, Goldstein et al. 2008, Cialdini and Schroeder 1976).** The principles are strategy, but the experiments underneath are literally wording tests: the same request with one clause changed, compliance counted. "Because" plus a reason lifted copier-line compliance from 60% to 94%. "Even a penny will help" roughly doubled donations. All tested and peer-reviewed, with mixed afterlives: the penny effect replicated and survived a meta-analysis, the Langer effect only holds for trivial requests, and the towel-norm study failed a direct replication. Each verdict is applied to its rule below.

**5. Eugene Schwartz, *Breakthrough Advertising*.** The canon's calibration dial: the five awareness stages dictate which nouns a headline may use, because the sentence that converts a ready buyer bounces a cold one. It teaches that word choice is a function of what the reader already knows, from full product-and-price naming at most-aware down to no product noun at all for the unaware. Asserted from mail-order experience. The framework was never a published dataset, but nothing in the tested literature contradicts it and modern funnel practice runs on it.

**6. Joseph Sugarman, *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook*.** The most granular sentence mechanics anywhere: the first sentence exists only to get the second read, so keep it near 3–6 words; end sagging paragraphs with a short forward-pulling fragment; vary sentence length on purpose; hunt every "that"; let the opening paragraphs set the buying environment. Asserted craft, validated only by his own P&L, but the rules are precise enough to falsify and they anticipate the fluency research by decades.

**7. Joanna Wiebe, Copyhackers case studies (Beachway, Wistia).** The biggest single language shift since the classics: stop authoring words and start curating them. Headlines get lifted verbatim from customer reviews, and drafts get a specificity sweep that swaps vague verbs for named features and numbers. Tested by live A/B runs with real numbers (Beachway review-mined headline: over 400% more button clicks and over 20% more form submits; Wistia rewrite: 3.5x paid conversions). Downgraded on scrutiny: both are single vendor-reported cases with no sample size or significance published, and three separate rules lean on the Wistia result alone. Strong hypotheses, thin proof.

**8. Claude Hopkins, *Scientific Advertising*.** The origin of the anti-platitude rule. Superlatives get read as "usual exaggeration" and discounted to zero, while a definite specific ("shaves in 78 seconds") is accepted as fact. Hopkins invented coupon-tracked split testing, so the method is tested, but the book reports conclusions without data. His "the more you tell, the more you sell" survives only as a conditioned heuristic, since no controlled modern evidence shows long copy reliably beating short.

**9. David Ogilvy, *Confessions of an Advertising Man* and *Ogilvy on Advertising*.** The register-setter. "The consumer is not a moron, she is your wife" converts respect into language rules: everyday words, factual density, no vapid adjectives. Mixed evidence: he leaned on Gallup and Starch readership research plus mail-order results, but most rules are research-informed assertion, and his "headlines are read 5x more than body copy" figure is unsourced folklore whose direction NN/g eye-tracking later supported.

**10. The gain/loss framing meta-analyses (O'Keefe and Jensen 2007–2009; Gallagher and Updegraff 2012).** Kills a folk law with numbers. Across roughly 150 studies, the gain/loss difference is tiny: loss framing wins only for detection behaviors at r = -.04 (53 studies, N = 9,145), gain framing wins for prevention at r ≈ .08 (94 studies), and gain frames get slightly more processing. Fully tested and verified. Its lesson is deflationary: framing is a tie-breaker, and clarity dominates it by an order of magnitude.

**11. van Laer et al. 2014, narrative transportation meta-analysis (Journal of Consumer Research).** Quantifies which writable story features move readers: imaginable plot r = .29, verisimilitude r = .27, identifiable character r = .20. Transported readers generate fewer counter-arguments (r = -.20) while attitudes (r = .44) and intentions (r = .31) shift. Tested: 132 effect sizes from 76 articles. It is the effect-size warrant for carrying a contestable claim as a scene instead of a bullet.

**12. Sopory and Dillard 2002, metaphor meta-analysis (Human Communication Research).** Turns metaphor from taste into dosage. Overall, metaphor beats literal phrasing at a bare r = .07, but one novel, non-extended metaphor with a familiar target placed early reaches r = .42. The best-supported mechanism was superior organization: the metaphor gives the reader a filing frame. Tested, verified, with one caveat: the .42 cell is a small post-hoc subgroup, so treat the swing as direction rather than precision.

**13. The rhyme-as-reason pair: McGlone and Tofighbakhsh 2000 (Psychological Science) and Filkuková and Klempe 2013 (Scandinavian Journal of Psychology).** Identical meaning, different sound, different judged truth. Rhyming aphorisms were rated more accurate than meaning-matched flat versions, and the effect shrank when raters were warned to ignore the poetry. The 2013 replication moved it to ad slogans across three experiments: rhymes rated more persuasive and more trustworthy. Both tested. Both measure ratings rather than purchases, which is why the rules confine rhyme to the one slogan-length line.

**14. The sensory-language pair: Cascio Rizzo et al. 2023 (Journal of Consumer Research) and Valenzuela-Gálvez et al. (International Journal of Business Communication).** Sense words (buttery, crumbles, still warm) beat equally positive evaluative words because they imply firsthand use; the JCR paper shows the lift runs through inferred authenticity. The IJBC paper adds five live A/B tests on real products where one sensory descriptor lifted engagement 15.3 to 21.5 points, with smell and touch and taste as the unsaturated channels. Both tested. Outcomes are engagement rather than purchases, and the literature is young.

**15. Lowrey and Shrum 2007, phonetic symbolism (Journal of Consumer Research).** Raw vowel sounds carry meaning before semantics: front vowels connote small and fast, back vowels large and heavy, and preference ran about 2:1 for names whose vowel fit the product attribute. Tested with minimal-pair fictitious names and replicated across languages. Caveat: the outcome is stated lab preference, so sound symbolism is a naming tiebreaker rather than a conversion lever.

**16. Packard and Berger 2020, "Thinking of You" (Psychological Science).** Refines why "you" works: it succeeds as a pointer into the reader's own life rather than as a spotlight aimed at them, and spotlight-you can read as accusation. Second-person frequency predicted chart success across thousands of song lyrics, with experiments locating the mechanism in personal relevance. Tested, though the domain is lyrics, so the transfer to conversion copy is an inference.

**17. George Loewenstein 1994, "The Psychology of Curiosity" (Psychological Bulletin).** The theory under every curiosity headline: curiosity is felt deprivation over a specific gap, its intensity follows an inverted U, and total mystery produces no gap at all. Asserted as theory synthesizing prior experiments, with later lab support. It explains a tested fact it did not produce: Caples' observation that curiosity headlines fail about 12 times per success.

**18. Nielsen Norman Group microcopy guidelines (error messages, command names, learn-more links, placeholders).** The most word-level prescriptive source in UX research: banned words named outright (invalid, illegal, incorrect), a numeric reading level for error text (grade 7–8), a two-clause error structure, verb-first 2–4 word labels, and the ruling that a form's name may never live in text that vanishes. Grounded in decades of watching users fail in usability tests rather than in split runs. Observational, but at unmatched word-level specificity.

**19. Torrey Podmajersky, *Strategic Writing for UX*.** The only book-length treatment of microcopy as a language system: a voice chart pinning vocabulary and verbosity per surface, flows drafted as spoken conversations, and the cleanest editing criterion in the field, words that "pass unremembered" while helping the user through. Asserted from practice at Google and Microsoft, paired with an explicit chapter on measuring every line.

**20. Ann Handley, *Everybody Writes*.** The web-era restatement of plain-style discipline: jargon as "the chemical additives of writing," the ugly first draft followed by a ruthless clarity edit, and the calibration rule "assume the reader knows nothing, but don't assume the reader is stupid." Asserted from long editorial experience, with no split-test data, but its rules operationalize what the tested concreteness literature implies.

**21. Gary Halbert, *The Boron Letters*.** The pace coach: follow short punches with one long sentence, then reset; use plain words with high sensory charge; give "eye relief" with short paragraphs and white space; hand-copy winning ads so their rhythm enters your ear. Asserted from direct-mail conviction and example rather than published tests.

**22. Gary Bencivenga, Marketing Bullets.** The proof-language specialist: never make a claim one inch bigger than its evidence, run the reader's "yeah, sure" test after every sentence, and buy belief for big promises with conditional framing ("If you've got 20 minutes a month, I guarantee..."). Mixed evidence: his own split runs behind the headline findings, plus the Langer experiment imported for the "because" effect, which later verification cut down to small requests only.

**23. Carpenter 2013, "But You Are Free" meta-analysis (Communication Studies).** One added clause acknowledging the reader's freedom to refuse raised compliance across 42 studies (roughly 22,000 participants, r ≈ .13), and only when the decision is immediate. Tested, then downgraded: a 2023 re-examination flagged the largest-effect included studies as at risk of data fabrication, so the pooled size is an upper bound.

**24. Michael Aagaard, first-person CTA button tests (ContentVerve/Unbounce).** The canonical single-word experiment: "Start my free 30 day trial" versus "Start your free 30 day trial," everything else constant, +90% clicks to the payment page. Tested once. Downgraded: a single three-week run on one page with no published sample size, and the claimed 10–40% replications are vendor-blog anecdotes. First-person buttons remain a cheap thing to test; the number is folklore.

**25. Alter and Oppenheimer 2006, processing fluency and stocks (PNAS).** Pronounceability alone moved real money: fluently named stocks beat disfluent ones by about $112 per $1,000 on day one, decaying fast. Tested and accurately cited, then downgraded: samples were tiny (89 IPOs, 116 tickers), the effect fades as information arrives, and later work attributes much of it to fluent-named firms being genuinely better. Supports a read-aloud audit for coined names in cold copy and nothing stronger.

**26. HubSpot CTA dataset.** Personalized CTAs converted 202% better than generic across 330,000+ CTAs over six months. Large-N but observational vendor analytics: "smart" CTAs were shown to known contacts who convert better regardless of copy, and the vendor sells the feature. Downgraded to directionally plausible. Its sturdier lesson survives elsewhere: plain-text emails with strong wording match designed ones, so invest in words.

## Part 2: The Rules That Survived

### Word stock

- **Replace every superlative with a definite specific: a number, a time, a named mechanism.** Readers discount "best" and "world-class" to zero and accept "shaves in 78 seconds" as fact. Asserted classic doctrine from coupon-era practice, never formally tested, consistent with the concreteness literature. [Hopkins](https://growthsummary.com/book-summary/scientific-advertising/), [Ogilvy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_customer_is_not_a_moron)
- **When a genuine offer exists, use the tested pull-words plainly: "free", "new", "announcing", "how to".** (ad) Caples' split runs put plain self-interest words above clever synonyms; caveat: his multipliers (3-to-1 on a headline change) are unaudited legend-grade records, so trust the ranking and never quote the numbers. [Caples](https://maverickwords.com/4-copywriting-lessons-from-john-caples-tested-advertising-methods)
- **Mine the headline from customer reviews and paste the phrasing verbatim, with as few abstractions as possible.** Beachway's review-lifted headline beat the brand-promise control by over 400% on button clicks; caveat: a single unaudited agency case, and the real lead lift was the far smaller 20%+ on form submits. [Copyhackers](https://copyhackers.com/2014/10/amazon-review-mining/)
- **Run a specificity sweep: replace vague verbs ("learn more", "take action") with the exact behavior, the named feature, or a number.** The Wistia rewrite built on sweeps converted 3.5x; caveat: one vendor-reported test with no methodology published, so treat as a strong heuristic resting on n=1. [Copyhackers](https://copyhackers.com/how-to-be-specific/)
- **Restate every number on the reader's body scale before it ships.** "More fat than a bacon-and-eggs breakfast, a Big Mac with fries, and a steak dinner, combined" beats "37g of fat"; the underlying concreteness mechanisms are peer-reviewed even though the packaging is asserted. [Heath & Heath](https://engineerguy.com/white-papers/made-to-stick.htm)
- **Reach for smell, touch, and taste words before sight and sound words; one sensory descriptor per product claim.** Five live A/B tests: 15.3 to 21.5 point engagement lifts over non-sensory descriptors of the same products. Outcome is engagement rather than sales. [Valenzuela-Gálvez et al.](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/23294884241290221)
- **When coining a name, pick the vowel that matches the attribute: front vowels (i, ee) for small and fast, back vowels (a, o, u) for big and sturdy.** About 2:1 preference for congruent names, replicated across languages; caveat: lab preference for fictitious names, so use it as a tiebreaker. [Lowrey & Shrum](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/34/3/406/1798924)
- **Read every coined term aloud once; if the mouth stumbles, consider renaming.** (landing, cold copy) Downgraded: the stock-fluency effect behind this ($112 per $1,000 day one) came from tiny samples, decays fast, and later work explains much of it rationally, so this is a cheap audit rather than a law. [Alter & Oppenheimer](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0601071103)
- **In editorial and attention registers, one concrete negative word outpulls its positive twin.** (split: email subject lines and content headlines only; Caples' mail-order tests warn against gloomy framing of the offer itself) Each added negative word raised CTR 2.3% across 105,000 headline variations. [Robertson et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-023-01538-4)
- **Choose the emotion category: sadness words earn clicks, fear words lose them, anger words do nothing.** (editorial/click surfaces) Verified against the Upworthy corpus: sadness β = +0.006, fear β = -0.007, both p < .001; outcome is CTR rather than sales. [Robertson et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-023-01538-4)
- **Start every button and command label with a verb that names the outcome: "Create invoice", never "OK" or "Submit".** From decades of NN/g usability observation rather than split runs. [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ui-copy/)
- **On conversion CTAs, choose the value word over the task word: "Get your free demo" beats "Sign up".** (landing) CTA copy tests report up to 49% CTR gains, all from the vendor-anecdote tier of evidence. [ABTestCases](https://www.abtestcases.com/testcases/changing-the-button-text-from-signup-for-free-to-get-started-now/)
- **Use one word per concept across the whole surface; a synonym in microcopy reads as a different feature.** NN/g consistency rule, enforced in practice by Podmajersky's voice chart. [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ui-copy/), [Podmajersky](https://www.uxmatters.com/mt/archives/2020/02/book-review-strategic-writing-for-ux.php)

### Pace

- **Keep the first sentence under about 8 words; its only job is to get the second sentence read.** Sugarman's tested openers run 3–6 words ("It had to happen."). Asserted craft with decades of direct-response feedback behind it. [Sugarman](https://www.whiskeyandstardust.com/adweek-copywriting-handbook-book-notes)
- **Vary sentence length on purpose: short punches, then one long sentence, then reset.** Halbert's gearshift and Sugarman's slippery slide agree; both asserted from practice. [Halbert](https://grahammann.net/book-notes/the-boron-letters-gary-halbert)
- **End a sagging paragraph with a short forward-pulling fragment ("Let me explain."), at most once or twice per piece.** Sugarman's own warning: overused, it reads as manipulation. The mechanism matches Loewenstein's gap theory. [Sugarman](https://www.whiskeyandstardust.com/adweek-copywriting-handbook-book-notes), [Loewenstein](https://psychologyfanatic.com/information-gap-theory/)
- **Compress by cutting topics and never by cutting detail; length follows specificity.** 7 of 8 winning Wistia emails ran 2–3x longer than their controls yet converted 3.5x; caveat: one self-reported case. [Copyhackers](https://copyhackers.com/how-to-be-specific/)
- **One message per email: one topic, one CTA.** (email) The Wistia winner covered one feature where the control listed three; caveat: n=1 vendor case, and the popular +371%/+1617% single-CTA statistic was killed in verification (see below). [Copyhackers](https://copyhackers.com/how-to-be-specific/)
- **Use exactly one metaphor, make it novel, anchor it to something the reader already knows, place it early, then drop it.** Meta-analysis: metaphor overall r = .07, but that exact combination reaches r = .42; caveat: the .42 cell is a small post-hoc subgroup, so take the direction seriously and the magnitude loosely. [Sopory & Dillard](https://academic.oup.com/hcr/article-pdf/28/3/382/22337510/jhumcom0382.pdf)
- **When a claim would trigger counter-arguing as a bullet, carry it as a two-to-three sentence scene: one named person, actions in sequence, plausible detail.** Meta-analysis of 132 effects: imaginable plot r = .29, verisimilitude r = .27, and transportation cuts critical thoughts (r = -.20) while moving attitude (r = .44). [van Laer et al.](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/40/5/797/2907487)
- **Compress the page's core promise to proverb length, one compact line that forces a real trade-off.** (landing) "THE low-fare airline" as the decision-guiding core; asserted synthesis over peer-reviewed memory research. [Heath & Heath](https://engineerguy.com/white-papers/made-to-stick.htm)
- **Keep interactive labels to 2–4 words and strip articles; one word when the verb alone carries the action.** NN/g scannability guideline from usability observation. [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ui-copy/)

### Fillers

- **Attach "because" plus a concrete reason to asks; never leave a CTA as a bare imperative.** The reason clause is the one filler that earns its place. Caveat, downgraded: Langer's 94% vs 60% holds only for trivial requests; in the high-stakes condition the placebic reason gave no lift, so big asks need a real reason and small asks merely need the word. [Langer et al.](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/brain-wise/201310/the-power-of-the-word-because-to-get-people-to-do-stuff)
- **Strip buzzwords and jargon in a dedicated edit pass; draft ugly, then edit for clarity.** Handley: "the chemical additives of writing." Asserted editorial doctrine with no split-test data. [Handley](https://annhandley.com/13-writing-rules/)
- **Replace evaluative adjectives ("amazing", "premium") with sense-channel words the writer could only know firsthand ("buttery", "still warm").** The opinion word is filler; the sense word carries proof. Verified across thousands of sponsored posts plus experiments, mediated by perceived authenticity; outcome is engagement rather than purchases. [Cascio Rizzo et al.](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/50/4/810/7072465)
- **Length is a density question: long copy is fine for considered purchases if every line adds information; cut by the fewest-words test, starting with "that" clauses.** (landing) Caveat, downgraded: "long outsells short" is folklore built on uncontrolled 1920s coupon experience and selected Ogilvy campaigns; keep the conditioned version only. [Sugarman](https://www.whiskeyandstardust.com/adweek-copywriting-handbook-book-notes), [Ogilvy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_customer_is_not_a_moron)
- **Edit microcopy to pass unremembered: cut any word the user would savor or reread.** Podmajersky's standard, asserted from practice at Google and Microsoft. [Podmajersky](https://www.uxmatters.com/mt/archives/2020/02/book-review-strategic-writing-for-ux.php)

### Assumed context

- **Calibrate the headline's nouns to the reader's awareness stage: most-aware may see product and price, unaware may see no product noun at all.** Schwartz's five stages, asserted from mail-order experience and never a published dataset, but the load-bearing frame of modern funnel practice. [Schwartz](https://betweenthelinescopy.com/blog/stages-of-awareness/)
- **Cold traffic opens at problem-aware or below; warm surfaces may assume product awareness and go straight to objections and CTA.** (split: ad vs retargeting/pricing) Applied Schwartz, asserted. [Stage mapping](https://robpalmer.com/blog/eugene-schwartz-breakthrough-advertising-lessons)
- **Define every term of art at first use in the reader's register; assume the reader knows nothing and is not stupid.** The Wistia rewrite voiced the reader's question ("What on earth is a turnstile?") and answered it inline; Copyhackers targets roughly 6th-grade reading level. Wistia caveat applies (n=1). [Handley](https://annhandley.com/13-writing-rules/), [Copyhackers](https://copyhackers.com/how-to-be-specific/)
- **Build curiosity with two edges: state what the reader almost knows, then name the one missing piece, and start paying it off in the first sentence.** (email subject lines especially) Loewenstein's inverted U: total mystery produces no gap; theory with lab support rather than copy split runs. [Loewenstein](https://psychologyfanatic.com/information-gap-theory/)
- **Lead with self-interest stated as a concrete outcome; reach for curiosity-only lines last.** Caples: curiosity headlines fail about a dozen times per success; caveat: that ratio is his impression rather than a count, though the appeal ranking came from decades of split runs. [Caples](https://archive.org/stream/pdfy-hHdovzQ-RvFny9Cy/Tested%20Advertising%20Methods_djvu.txt)
- **Write every headline to stand alone with no help from the body.** Caveat: Caples' "scores of people read only headlines" is rhetoric rather than measurement, but NN/g eye-tracking independently confirms that most readers scan. [Caples](https://archive.org/stream/pdfy-hHdovzQ-RvFny9Cy/Tested%20Advertising%20Methods_djvu.txt)
- **A metaphor's target must already live in the reader's head; map the new thing onto a known thing.** The familiar-target condition was required for the r = .42 effect, and superior organization was the best-supported mechanism of six tested. [Sopory & Dillard](https://academic.oup.com/hcr/article-pdf/28/3/382/22337510/jhumcom0382.pdf)
- **Write social proof as what people exactly like the reader actually do ("teams your size"), never a global brag.** (landing) Caveat, downgraded: the towel study behind the numbers (35% to 44%, to 49% localized) failed a direct German replication where the standard message won; keep the framing as a hypothesis to test, never quote the lifts. [Goldstein et al.](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/35/3/472/1856257)
- **Every error message carries two clauses in order: exactly what happened, then the lowest-effort fix.** Never a bare "An error occurred." NN/g usability observation. [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/error-message-guidelines/)
- **Error text reads at grade 7–8 or lower; codes hide behind a details affordance.** [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/error-message-guidelines/)
- **Never label a link "Learn More" or "Click here"; front-load the destination keyword so the label survives out of context.** Generic labels strand scanners and screen-reader users. [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/learn-more-links/)
- **Label controls with the state the click produces ("Pause" while playing); an ellipsis means more input is coming.** [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ui-copy/)
- **The field's name lives in a permanent visible label; placeholder text may only hint at format.** Seven documented failure modes for disappearing labels, including broken error recovery. [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/form-design-placeholders/)

### Opinion and certainty

- **Size every claim to its proof, never an inch bigger; run the "yeah, sure" test after each sentence and answer it in the next.** Conditional framing buys belief for big promises ("If you've got 20 minutes a month, I guarantee..."); Bencivenga's own split runs, unpublished. [Bencivenga](https://nordiccopy.com/gary-bencivengas-marketing-bullets-a-summary/)
- **State facts flatly, without hedging adverbs and without hype intensifiers; certainty comes from specificity and proof.** Asserted classic doctrine. [Ogilvy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_customer_is_not_a_moron), [Hopkins](https://growthsummary.com/book-summary/scientific-advertising/)
- **Voice the claim as the reader's own verdict rather than the brand's promise.** "If you think you need rehab, you do" beat "Your Addiction Ends Here"; caveat: one agency case, and the 400%+ figure is button clicks while the lead lift was the smaller 20%+. [Copyhackers](https://copyhackers.com/2014/10/amazon-review-mining/)
- **Default outcome sentences to the gain frame; switch to loss framing only for check-and-find-out actions, and never distort a plain claim to force either frame.** Verified meta-analytic numbers: loss advantage r = -.04 (detection only, 53 studies, N = 9,145); gain advantage r ≈ .08 for prevention. Framing is a tie-breaker; concreteness dominates. [O'Keefe & Jensen](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1460-2466.2009.01417.x), [Gallagher & Updegraff](https://academic.oup.com/abm/article-abstract/43/1/101/4563944)
- **Use exact figures instead of round claims: "$35,840 in 2 years" reads as a measurement, a round number reads as an opinion.** Caples doctrine from split-run practice; caveat: his specific winning multipliers are legend-grade. [Caples](https://archive.org/stream/pdfy-hHdovzQ-RvFny9Cy/Tested%20Advertising%20Methods_djvu.txt)
- **Make at least one claim per surface reader-testable in wording ("check your last invoice") rather than asking to be believed.** Heath testable-credential pathway; asserted synthesis. [Heath & Heath](https://engineerguy.com/white-papers/made-to-stick.htm)
- **Give the one must-believe line (tagline, subject line) rhyme or tight rhythm, and keep evidence lines plain.** Verified twice: rhyming lines are judged more accurate and more trustworthy at identical meaning, and the effect vanishes under scrutiny, so never rhyme the fine print, and only rhyme claims you can back. Documented at slogan length only. [McGlone & Tofighbakhsh](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/1467-9280.00282), [Filkuková & Klempe](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/sjop.12069)
- **When two headline drafts differ by one word, that difference is worth an A/B; the winner is not guessable from craft intuition.** Per-word effects of about 2.3% CTR were invisible to Upworthy's own editors before testing (32,487 experiments, 538M assignments). [Upworthy archive](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-021-00934-7)
- **No jokes in messages users may hit repeatedly; humor reads as evasion on first encounter and contempt on the fifth.** [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/error-message-guidelines/)
- **State what the system did in plain declaratives ("Saved"); a hedge in a status message transfers uncertainty the user cannot act on.** [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/error-message-guidelines/)

### Relationship

- **Write as one competent salesman speaking to one intelligent adult, in the words she uses every day; never advertising voice.** Ogilvy's 1955 register rule plus Hopkins' salesman-in-print frame; asserted, load-bearing, uncontradicted. [Ogilvy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_customer_is_not_a_moron)
- **Give eye relief: short paragraphs, generous breaks, one vivid metaphor doing a paragraph's work.** (landing) Formatting says "this will be easy for you." Halbert, asserted. [Halbert](https://grahammann.net/book-notes/the-boron-letters-gary-halbert)
- **Prose addresses the reader as "you"; the button flips to first person ("Start my free trial") so the click reads as the user's own sentence.** (split: landing pages; the flip applies to the button only) Caveat, downgraded: the +90% Aagaard result is a single unreplicated three-week test and the claimed 10–40% replications are anecdotes, so treat first-person buttons as a cheap test rather than a law. [Aagaard/Kissmetrics](https://kissmetrics.io/blog/cta-button-best-practices)
- **Write "you" as a pointer into the reader's own life ("the first thing you smell when you open the box") and avoid spotlight-you commands ("you need this"), which read as accusation.** Verified in lyrics corpora plus experiments; caveat: the domain is songs, so the transfer to copy is an inference. [Packard & Berger](https://faculty.wharton.upenn.edu/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/You.pdf)
- **Personalize the ask itself, and pay off the subject line's promise in the same words end to end.** (email) Caveat, downgraded: HubSpot's 202% figure (330,000+ CTAs) is observational vendor analytics confounded by audience selection; keep message match, distrust the number. [HubSpot](https://blog.hubspot.com/blog/tabid/6307/bid/32606/the-9-must-have-components-of-compelling-email-copy.aspx)
- **Close the ask with an autonomy clause ("but it's your call") in the same breath as an act-now CTA; drop it when the decision can't be made immediately.** (email) Caveat, downgraded: the r ≈ .13 across 42 studies is an upper bound, since a 2023 re-examination flagged the largest-effect studies as at risk of fabrication. [Carpenter](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/A-Meta-Analysis-of-the-Effectiveness-of-the-%E2%80%9CBut-Carpenter/5ad367ae86a6266e13e1f484d11ecf37cde82384)
- **Name the minimum viable action explicitly ("even five minutes helps", "start with one page").** Verified with replications and a supportive meta-analysis: "even a penny will help" lifted donation compliance from 28.6% to 50% without shrinking average gifts. [Cialdini & Schroeder 1976](https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.34.4.599)
- **Attach sensory words only to what the brand can plausibly have touched; a sense word implies firsthand use, and a faked one reverses the authenticity gain.** (landing) Verified mediation result: the entire lift runs through inferred authenticity. [Cascio Rizzo et al.](https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article-abstract/50/4/810/7072465)
- **Delete anything that makes the reader admire the writing; if the reaction is "how clever" instead of "I want that", the line tested worse.** Caples' split-run verdict, direction credible, numbers legend-grade. [Caples](https://archive.org/stream/pdfy-hHdovzQ-RvFny9Cy/Tested%20Advertising%20Methods_djvu.txt)
- **Set the register in the first two paragraphs deliberately; the opening is the buying environment, so premium copy cannot open in bargain-bin words.** (landing) Sugarman, asserted. [Sugarman](https://www.whiskeyandstardust.com/adweek-copywriting-handbook-book-notes)
- **Ban blame vocabulary in errors ("invalid", "illegal", "incorrect", "failed"); frame the message around the system's limit.** [NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/error-message-guidelines/)
- **Draft any flow as a spoken two-party conversation first, then keep only the lines a human helper would say aloud.** Podmajersky's conversation mapping, asserted from practice. [Podmajersky](https://ellessmedia.com/csi/torrey-podmajersky/)

### Checked and killed

- **"Single-CTA emails get +371% clicks and +1617% sales."** Killed: not a study; the figures trace to one marketer's passing anecdote about one campaign, repeated by WordStream and then by hundreds of blogs citing WordStream citing nothing. The one-topic-one-email rule above stands on other (thin) evidence; this number pair is a zombie statistic and must not be cited.
