# copy

Website and marketing copy: landing pages, product pages, launch posts, ads, sales email, microcopy. Persuasive without sleaze. The full evidence base is `references/copy-canon.md` (26 ranked sources, 53 verified rules); the humanness research is `references/research.md`. Arranged on the framework spine (`references/framework.md`). Where a rule differs by sub-register it says so: (landing), (email), (ad), (micro).

## word stock

- Replace every superlative with a definite specific: a number, a time, a named mechanism. Readers discount "best" to zero and accept "shaves in 78 seconds" as fact.
- Picturable words over category words: "grey jeans" beats "pants". One SD more concreteness raised satisfaction 8.9% and spending 10 to 13%, and it only works when it's relevant to what the reader came for.
- Mine phrasing from customer reviews and paste it near-verbatim; curate words instead of authoring them. Run a specificity sweep: vague verbs ("learn more") become the exact behavior or a number.
- Restate every number on the reader's body scale: "more fat than a bacon-and-eggs breakfast, a Big Mac with fries, and a steak dinner, combined" beats "37g of fat".
- Sense words over evaluation words: "buttery", "still warm" carry proof; "amazing" is filler. Smell, touch, and taste beat sight and sound (15 to 21 point engagement lifts). Only attach a sense word to what the brand plausibly touched; a faked one reverses the gain.
- Use "is". AI rewrites "is" into "serves as". The copula is confident.
- (ad) When a genuine offer exists, the tested pull-words plainly: "free", "new", "how to". Trust Caples' ranking, never quote his multipliers.
- (micro) Buttons start with the outcome verb: "Create invoice", never "Submit". One word per concept across the whole surface; a synonym in microcopy reads as a different feature.
- Banned: advertisement adjectives (vibrant, rich, nestled, renowned, world-class), puffery, "whether you're a X or a Y", fake urgency. Plain words can repeat; synonym-cycling reads machine.
- Signature phrases: none by default, that slot belongs to the brand. Jargon budget: zero unglossed terms; the reader's own domain words are free.

## pace

- First sentence under about 8 words. Its only job is to get the second sentence read.
- Sentences average 15 to 20 words, split past 30, vary on purpose: long sets up, short lands. End a sagging paragraph with a short forward-pulling fragment ("Let me explain.") at most once or twice per piece.
- Grade 6 to 8, never past 12. 79% of web readers scan: front-load meaning, structure for the F-pattern. Headings and bullets earn their keep here.
- Compress by cutting topics, never detail. Length follows specificity; "long outsells short" is folklore, density is the real rule.
- (landing) Compress the core promise to proverb length, one compact line that forces a real trade-off.
- (email) One topic, one CTA per email.
- (micro) Labels 2 to 4 words, articles stripped, edited to pass unremembered.

## fillers

- Everyday transitions keep it moving like talk: "Well," "anyway," "now". Roughly two per page, never two per paragraph. Bare openers yes, sparingly ("Look,"). Final-position markers off; pages don't yield turns.
- Attach "because" plus a concrete reason to every ask. Small asks merely need the word; big asks need a real reason (the placebic version only ever worked on trivial requests).
- Strip buzzwords in a dedicated pass. Draft ugly, edit for clarity.
- No information-state markers; someone wrote this page on purpose.

## assumed context

- Calibrate the nouns to the reader's awareness stage: most-aware may see product and price, unaware may see no product noun at all. (ad) Cold traffic opens problem-aware or below; warm surfaces go straight to objections and CTA.
- Open on the reader's situation, never on the product. The first sentence is about their Tuesday.
- Every headline stands alone with no help from the body; most readers scan.
- Lead with self-interest as a concrete outcome; curiosity-only lines last. Curiosity works with two edges: what the reader almost knows, then the one missing piece, paid off starting in the first sentence.
- Define every term of art at first use in the reader's register. Assume the reader knows nothing and is not stupid.
- Social proof as what people exactly like the reader do ("teams your size"), never a global brag. Treat the lift numbers as hypotheses; the famous towel study failed replication.
- (micro) Errors: exactly what happened, then the lowest-effort fix. Grade 7 to 8. No bare "an error occurred", no "Learn more" links.

## opinion and certainty

- Size every claim to its proof, never an inch bigger. Run the "yeah, sure" test after each sentence and answer it in the next.
- Exact figures over round ones: "$35,840 in 2 years" reads as measurement, a round number reads as opinion. Every number gets a source.
- Make at least one claim reader-testable in wording ("check your last invoice") instead of asking to be believed.
- Voice the claim as the reader's own verdict, in their words, rather than the brand's promise.
- Default to the gain frame; loss framing only for check-and-find-out actions. Framing is a tie-breaker (r about .08 at best); concreteness dominates it by an order of magnitude.
- Negativity splits by surface: (email subjects, content headlines) one concrete negative word outpulls its positive twin, sadness pulls, fear repels, anger does nothing (2.3% CTR per negative word, 105k headline variations). The offer itself stays honestly framed, never gloomy. Honest limits stated as facts ("takes an hour to set up") build trust and are not hedges; hedging on your own product is banned.
- Rhyme or tight rhythm on the one must-believe line only (tagline, subject); evidence lines stay plain. The effect is real at slogan length and vanishes under scrutiny.
- Intensity ceiling: praise stays concrete or stays out. The moment an adjective could apply to any product, it's puffery.
- When two drafts differ by one word, that's worth an A/B; per-word effects were invisible to Upworthy's own editors across 32,487 experiments. 5 to 25% swings from headline and CTA changes are commonly reported. Judgment starts the draft, behavior finishes it.

## relationship

- One competent person speaking to one intelligent adult, in the words she uses every day. Never advertising voice. Draft it as a letter to someone specific; keep only what you'd say across a table.
- The register blend that works: involvement (you, contractions, private verbs) mixed with overt persuasion (imperatives, "if", will/should/must). The impersonal informational default is the wrong pole here.
- "You" as a pointer into the reader's own life ("the first thing you smell when you open the box"), never spotlight-you commands ("you need this"), which read as accusation.
- (landing) The button may flip to first person ("Start my free trial") so the click reads as the user's own sentence; a cheap test worth running, never a law. The CTA is one imperative sentence, value word over task word ("Get your free demo" beats "Sign up").
- Name the minimum viable action: "even five minutes helps", "start with one page". Verified with replications ("even a penny" lifted compliance 28.6% to 50% without shrinking gifts).
- (email) Pay off the subject line's promise in the same words end to end. An autonomy clause ("but it's your call") may close an act-now ask; drop it when the decision can't be made now.
- (landing) Eye relief: short paragraphs, generous breaks. Formatting says "this will be easy for you." Set the register in the first two paragraphs; the opening is the buying environment.
- Delete anything that makes the reader admire the writing. If the reaction is "how clever" instead of "I want that", the line tested worse.
- (micro) No jokes in messages users hit repeatedly. No blame vocabulary in errors ("invalid", "failed"); frame around the system's limit. Status messages are plain declaratives ("Saved").
- Peer voice, warm but never servile.

## sample

Before (AI): "Unlock the power of effortless collaboration. Our robust, seamless platform empowers teams to work smarter in today's fast-paced digital landscape. Join thousands of forward-thinking companies on their journey to productivity."

After (human): "Your team runs one project through five tools. The brief's in email, the files are in some chat thread nobody can find. Put it on one board instead. If a due date moves, the calendar moves. Slack and Drive plug in, so you keep the workflow you have. Start free, invite two people, see if Tuesday feels different."
