# copy

Website and marketing copy: landing pages, product pages, launch posts, ads. Persuasive without sleaze. The evidence base is in `references/research.md`; the craft leg is Halbert's Boron Letters and Ogilvy's 1982 memo ("Write the way you talk. Naturally.").

## The register

Effective copy mixes two things AI keeps separate: involvement (you, contractions, private verbs) and overt persuasion (imperatives, "if", will/should/must). A study of 225 top-brand texts found that blend is the signature of ads that work. The impersonal informational default is the wrong pole here.

- Address the reader as "you". Second-person raises involvement and brand attitude via self-reference.
- Write as one person to one person. Draft it as a letter to someone specific, keep only what you'd say across a table.
- Everyday transitions keep it moving like talk: "Well," "anyway," "now". Halbert used them about twice a letter.
- Open on the reader's situation, never on the product. The first sentence is about their Tuesday.

## Concreteness sells, measured

One standard deviation more concrete language raised customer satisfaction 8.9% and spending 10 to 13% in a 1,000+ interaction study. The mechanism: concrete words prove you listened. Two rules ride on that:

- Picturable words over category words: "grey jeans" beats "pants", "the 6mm hex on the seat post" beats "components".
- Concreteness only works when it's relevant to what the reader came for. Concrete-but-random reads as noise.

## Reading mechanics

- Grade 6 to 8 for general audiences, never past 12. Experts prefer plain language too; the study had a professor and an IT manager both picking the plainer versions.
- 79% of web readers scan. Front-load meaning, structure for the F-pattern, make the fraction they read the right fraction. This is the one register where headings and bullets earn their keep.
- Sentences average 15 to 20 words, split anything past 30, and vary on purpose: long sets up, short lands.
- Plain words can repeat. Swapping synonyms to sound varied moves text toward the machine profile (grade 16.6 vs the human 12.1 in the essay corpus).

## Honesty as style

- Use "is". AI rewrites "is" into "serves as" and "stands as". The copula is confident.
- Cut advertisement adjectives: vibrant, rich, nestled, renowned, world-class. A concrete claim or nothing.
- Every number gets a source. "Up to 40%" with no citation is the machine's move and the reader smells it.
- Keep the honest negative. "Setup takes an hour and the first print will probably fail" builds more trust than uniform enthusiasm, because relentless positivity is a statistical tell and readers dock warmth from text they suspect is AI.
- Test copy where you can; 5 to 25% conversion swings from headline and CTA changes are commonly reported. Judgment starts the draft, behavior finishes it.

## Banned in this tone

Beyond the universal core: puffery (revolutionary, cutting-edge, game-changing, unleash), "whether you're a X or a Y", "in today's fast-paced world", fake urgency, invented statistics, and the five-paragraph shape. The CTA is one imperative sentence.

## Sample

Before (AI): "Unlock the power of effortless collaboration. Our robust, seamless platform empowers teams to work smarter in today's fast-paced digital landscape. Join thousands of forward-thinking companies on their journey to productivity."

After (human): "Your team runs one project through five tools. The brief's in email, the files are in some chat thread nobody can find. Put it on one board instead. If a due date moves, the calendar moves. Slack and Drive plug in, so you keep the workflow you have. Start free, invite two people, see if Tuesday feels different."
