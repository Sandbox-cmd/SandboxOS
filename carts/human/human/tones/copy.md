# copy

Website and marketing copy: landing pages, product pages, launch posts, ads. Persuasive without sleaze. Evidence in `references/research.md`; the craft leg is Halbert's Boron Letters and Ogilvy's 1982 memo ("Write the way you talk. Naturally."). Arranged on the framework spine (`references/framework.md`).

## word stock

- Picturable words over category words: "grey jeans" beats "pants", "the 6mm hex on the seat post" beats "components". One standard deviation more concreteness raised satisfaction 8.9% and spending 10 to 13% in a 1,000+ interaction study, because concrete words prove you listened. Concreteness only works when it's relevant to what the reader came for.
- Use "is". AI rewrites "is" into "serves as" and "stands as". The copula is confident.
- Banned: advertisement adjectives (vibrant, rich, nestled, renowned, world-class), puffery (revolutionary, cutting-edge, game-changing, unleash), "whether you're a X or a Y", "in today's fast-paced world", fake urgency.
- Plain words can repeat. Synonym-swapping to sound varied moves text toward the machine profile (grade 16.6 vs the human 12.1 in the essay corpus).
- Signature phrases: none by default. That slot belongs to the brand using the tone.
- Jargon budget: zero unglossed terms for general audiences. The reader's own domain words are free; yours cost a gloss.

## pace

- Grade 6 to 8 for general audiences, never past 12. Experts prefer plain language too.
- Sentences average 15 to 20 words, split anything past 30, and vary on purpose: long sets up, short lands.
- 79% of web readers scan. Front-load meaning, structure for the F-pattern, make the fraction they read the right fraction. This is the one register where headings and bullets earn their keep.

## fillers

- Everyday transitions keep it moving like talk: "Well," "anyway," "now". Halbert used them about twice a letter. That's the dose: roughly two per page, never two per paragraph.
- Bare openers: yes, sparingly. "Look," and "Now," at a pivot, Halbert-style.
- Final-position markers: off. Pages don't yield turns.
- No information-state markers; this is still a page, someone wrote it on purpose.

## assumed context

- Open on the reader's situation, never on the product. The first sentence is about their Tuesday.
- Address the reader as "you". Second-person raises involvement and brand attitude via self-reference.
- Write as one person to one person. Draft it as a letter to someone specific, keep only what you'd say across a table.
- Assume the reader's world, gloss your own: their problem needs no introduction, your product does.

## opinion and certainty

- Keep the honest negative. "Setup takes an hour and the first print will probably fail" builds more trust than uniform enthusiasm, because relentless positivity is a statistical tell and readers dock warmth from text they suspect is AI.
- Every number gets a source. "Up to 40%" with no citation is the machine's move and the reader smells it.
- Commit: the page recommends, plainly. Hedging on your own product is banned; it reads as either dishonesty or doubt. Honest limits stated as facts ("takes an hour to set up") are not hedges.
- Intensity ceiling: praise stays concrete or stays out. The moment an adjective could apply to any product, it's puffery.

## relationship

- The register blend that works: involvement (you, contractions, private verbs) mixed with overt persuasion (imperatives, "if", will/should/must). A study of 225 top-brand texts found that blend is the signature of ads that work. The impersonal informational default is the wrong pole here.
- Peer voice, warm but never servile. The CTA is one imperative sentence.
- Test copy where you can; 5 to 25% conversion swings from headline and CTA changes are commonly reported. Judgment starts the draft, behavior finishes it.

## sample

Before (AI): "Unlock the power of effortless collaboration. Our robust, seamless platform empowers teams to work smarter in today's fast-paced digital landscape. Join thousands of forward-thinking companies on their journey to productivity."

After (human): "Your team runs one project through five tools. The brief's in email, the files are in some chat thread nobody can find. Put it on one board instead. If a due date moves, the calendar moves. Slack and Drive plug in, so you keep the workflow you have. Start free, invite two people, see if Tuesday feels different."
